PIC Guardrail Preparation

Product Innovation and Management — MKTG1092, Week 4 — Activity 4.9

What this form is for: This activity consolidates everything you have produced in Activities 4.6, 4.7, and 4.8 into five evidence-based guardrail inputs for your Product Innovation Charter (PIC). You are not writing the PIC here — you are assembling the decisions and evidence that will let you write it quickly and confidently in Week 5. You will also write your opportunity statement and the opportunity framing summary required by Assessment 1.
Before you begin — have all four of these documents open:

  • Activity 4.7 Competitor Gap and White-Space Map export — feeds into Guardrail 2
  • Activity 4.8 Consumer Usage, Occasions and Pain Points export — feeds into Guardrails 1, 3, and 4
  • Week 3 Opportunity Direction export — feeds into Guardrails 4 and 5

Brand and category context

Guardrail 1

Who we serve

Describe your target consumer segment and the key job they are trying to get done. Connect your consumer definition to the specific occasions and pain points that make this consumer the right target for your opportunity. One to two sentences drawn from your Activity 4.8 export.

Combine your consumer segment definition (Activity 4.8, Section 1) with the most important JTBD statement (Activity 4.8, Section 4) into one to two connected sentences.

Guardrail 2

Where we will play

Name the product segment and the white-space position within it that your innovation will target. This is the competitive address of your concept — specific enough that someone reading it could identify exactly where on the competitive map you intend to sit. One to two sentences drawn from Activities 4.6 and 4.7.

Combine your segment choice (Activity 4.6, Section 3) with your white-space position (Activity 4.7, Section 4) into one to two connected sentences.

Guardrail 3

Value we must deliver

State the non-negotiable benefits the concept must deliver for the consumer. These are drawn directly from your Activity 4.8 non-negotiables. Keep them measurable and consumer-facing — not technical specifications, but the outcomes the consumer must experience. One to two sentences.

Draw from your non-negotiable requirements (Activity 4.8, Section 5). List the two or three most important requirements and explain why they are non-negotiable by linking them to specific pain points or JTBD statements.

Guardrail 4

Feasibility guardrails

Describe the design and technology constraints, the estimated timeline, and any capability or cost dependencies that bound what is achievable. Draw on your Week 3 technology scan and your Activity 4.8 non-negotiables. Be practical — name the specific resources or capabilities required and note any dependencies you cannot yet confirm. One to two sentences.

Combine relevant technology signals (Week 3 Opportunity Direction export) with the feasibility constraints implied by your non-negotiables (Activity 4.8, Section 5). Name the main technical or commercial dependency and the estimated timeframe to first launch.

Guardrail 5

Risks and watch-outs

State the two biggest risks to this opportunity — the external factors or competitive threats that could undermine it — and the first mitigation step for each. Draw on your Activity 4.7 competitor analysis and your Activity 4.6 segment assessment. One to two sentences per risk.

Name the risk, explain why it matters, and state one concrete first step that reduces it. Draw from Activity 4.7 (competitive threats) or Activity 4.6 (segment intensity).

Opportunity statement and framing summary

These two outputs go directly into Assessment 1. Read the Assessment 1 brief before writing them — the brief specifies exactly what each section must contain. Draft them here so you can refine them before submission.

State who you are serving, what the product concept is at a high level, and which market position you are targeting. This is the headline of your innovation brief. It should be specific enough to distinguish your concept from any other and broad enough to leave design options open.

Sentence 1: describe the market context — the category, the segment, and the evidence that the opportunity is real and timely. Sentence 2: describe the target consumer and the specific unmet need your innovation addresses. Sentence 3: state how your brand will win in this space and why the timing is right. This is the concluding section of Assessment 1.

List two to three sources that underpin the market claims in your opportunity framing. These may include market reports, consumer research, or industry publications. Harvard format. See the RMIT EasyCite guide if needed.
